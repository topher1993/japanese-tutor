## 1. Tier 1 Mandatory Icons — Exact Asset List

This is the floor. Without these, the app cannot ship to either store. Everything else is nice-to-have. We are picking the exact files we owe Kaisel, no optional extras.

### 1.1 Master icon — `assets/icon/icon-1024.png`
- **Size:** 1024 × 1024 px, square, no rounded corners, no transparency on background.
- **Format:** PNG, sRGB, no alpha.
- **Purpose:** Source of truth. Every other icon is downscaled from this (or its parts).
- **Design rules baked in (Play Store + Apple HIG):**
  - No text smaller than what reads at 60 px (Apple: "test at small sizes").
  - No photographic faces — Play Store rejects likenesses of public figures; Apple rejects photo-realistic faces in icons.
  - Single dominant focal element, high contrast, recognizable in 1 second at 29 px (Settings grid size on iOS).
  - Avoid embedding the wordmark in the icon; the wordmark lives in splash and store listing.
  - Corners will be auto-masked by both stores, so the safe zone is the inner 824 × 824 circle (iOS "icon grid") — keep the focal element inside that.

### 1.2 iOS App Store variants — `assets/icon/ios/`
Generated by `npx expo prebuild` / `eas-build` from a single source, but we list them explicitly because each is a separate upload to App Store Connect (Xcode will accept an `icon-1024.png` only; the rest are auto-derived from `Contents.json`). What we owe:
- `icon-1024.png` — 1024 × 1024, no alpha, no rounded corners. **This is the only one we author; Expo generates the rest from `app.json` `ios.icon`.**
- **App Store Connect upload:** the 1024 above only. Do not hand-craft the 180/167/152/120/87/80/60/58/40/29 sizes — `expo prebuild` produces `Assets.xcassets/AppIcon.appiconset/*.png` from the single 1024 source.
- **iOS HIG requirement cited:** "App Icon" section of the Human Interface Guidelines — "Provide a 1024 × 1024 px app icon. Icons should be the same artwork, with no transparency, in the square shape with no rounded corners." Apple's Icon Grid also defines the inner safe zone (~824 px circle).

### 1.3 Android adaptive icon — `assets/icon/android/`
Required since API 26+ (Android 8.0, 2017). Play Store listing still accepts a legacy 512 × 512, but the adaptive parts are mandatory for the in-app launcher.
- `android/icon-foreground.png` — **1080 × 1080 px**, PNG, transparent background. Safe zone is the inner 720 × 720 circle; outer 1080 is the bleed for parallax/parallax-effect launchers. Keep mascot/focal inside 720.
- `android/icon-background.png` — **1080 × 1080 px**, PNG, no transparency. Solid or gradient using brand palette only (recommend `#2A6F97` flat, or a soft vertical gradient `#2A6F97` → `#014F86`).
- `android/icon-monochrome.png` — **1080 × 1080 px**, PNG, transparent background, **single solid color (white) on transparency**. This is the Android 13+ themed icon. The system tints it to match the user's wallpaper. The art should be the foreground silhouette only, no color, no shading.
- `android/adaptive-icon-foreground.png` — legacy alias for some launchers, same as foreground.
- (Optional but cheap) `android/icon-512.png` — legacy Play Store listing icon, 512 × 512. We include it because it's a one-line export.

**Play Store spec cited:** Android Developers → "Adaptive icon support" and Play Console upload help: "high-res icon 512 × 512, plus adaptive icon foreground 1088 × 1088 with safe zone." (We use 1080 because that is the modern spec; Expo's `expo-build-properties` accepts 1080.)

### 1.4 Splash / launch icon — `assets/splash/`
Expo splits this into two layers:
- `splash.png` — background image, **1284 × 2778 px** (iPhone 15 Pro Max safe size) or simpler **1242 × 2436 px** if we want one asset for all. Solid `#E0F2FE` (brandSoft) background, or a very subtle vertical gradient `#E0F2FE` → `#FFFFFF`.
- `icon.png` (in splash) — **1024 × 1024** the mascot mark, used centered on splash. Same as the focal element of the master icon, on transparent.
- `assets/splash/*.png` per `app.json` `splash.image` resolution. Expo will downscale from one 1242×2436 source.

**Apple HIG cited:** "Launch Screen" → "Avoid including text or content that looks like an ad; use a static, simple image that resembles the first screen of your app." Splash is a launch screen, not a marketing surface — keep it calm.

## 2. Onboarding Illustration Plan (4 steps)

**Decisive tone pick: calm + quietly warm.** Not playful, not professional. Justification: the user is a foreign worker in Japan, probably on a phone at 6:30am before a shift. They are not in the mood for a cartoon. Duolingo's playful tone works for hobbyists; Babbel's adult/calm tone works for pragmatists. Our user is a pragmatist who is also nervous. Calm + warm is the right register.

### 2.1 Mascot (appears in all 4 onboarding illustrations + empty states + splash)

- **Name suggestion:** **"Kō" (コウ)** — short, two-stroke kana that any N5 learner recognizes (the character 子 or simply the katakana コ are both N5-vocabulary adjacent; 子 is in N5 kanji list for "child/sign of the rat"). Pick the hiragana **こ** as the on-mascot mark — the very first hiragana most learners meet. This is a teaching opportunity baked into the brand.
- **Look:** A single soft **rounded droplet/seed shape** with a tiny face (two dot eyes, no mouth) and a small **circular amber dot** on the "cheek" area (like a temperature pip). That amber dot doubles as the brand accent and as a visual reference to a "tab" or "marker" — useful for flashcard UI later.
- **Why minimalist:** One body shape. No arms, no legs, no fingers. The amber dot is the only detail besides the face. It is a **flat single-color silhouette** in `#2A6F97` (brand) with a single `#F4A261` (amber) accent dot. Two colors total. This is the spec. We do not add a third color.
- **Pose logic:** Because it has no limbs, "doing" things is expressed by **props it sits next to or in front of**: a helmet, a card, a calendar, a speech bubble. The mascot is a still figure; the props do the storytelling. This is the budget-friendly way to keep the mascot recognizably the same across four scenes.
- **What it never does:** never wears a uniform of a specific industry (would alienate users in other industries), never holds a phone (we are inside the phone), never has a mouth (mouths make faces hard to keep consistent at small sizes).

### 2.2 Step 1 — Welcome
- **Illustration needed?** **Yes.** First impression sets expectations. Empty hero area is a wasted opportunity.
- **Scene:** Kō sits on a large rounded **speech bubble card** that contains the kanji **日本語** (Japanese language) and a small hiragana **にほんご** line under it. Around the speech bubble, three tiny floating speech bubbles with single N5 words we know exist: **「あ」** (a), **「い」** (i), **「う」** (u) — the first three vowels. No invented characters.
- **Props:** one large speech bubble (the stage), three tiny floating bubbles. Background: solid `#E0F2FE`.
- **Why this scene:** puts the mascot, the language, and a familiar visual metaphor (speech bubble) on screen in the first 800 ms.

### 2.3 Step 2 — Language pick (UI vs. native target)
- **Illustration needed?** **No.** The step is a **list of two options** (UI language and target language). An illustration behind a picker is noise. Replace with a calm typographic layout and a small mascot **inline badge** in the top-right corner (24 × 24 px, just the Kō head silhouette in brand color). This is a deliberate decision to save one illustration for higher-value steps.
- **Compensation:** keep the inline mascot badge consistent so the character still has presence.

### 2.4 Step 3 — Workplace goal
- **Illustration needed?** **Yes.** This is the step that personalizes the app. We want a calm, grounding moment.
- **Scene:** Kō sits on top of a small **stack of three rounded workplace cards** (the kinds of cards the app will teach). The top card shows a workplace-relevant N5 phrase that exists in our packs: **「しごと」** (work, hiragana) with a small icon of a hard hat (we use a hard hat as a generic workplace marker — most N5 packs include 仕事 and 安全 / あんぜん). The amber dot on Kō's cheek aligns with the top card to suggest "this is yours." Background: solid `#E0F2FE`. A subtle dashed path leads from the cards off-screen to the right, implying progress.
- **Props:** three stacked cards, one hard hat icon on the top card. All symbols from the N5 pack.
- **Why this scene:** anchors the app's purpose (workplace Japanese) without making a specific industry claim.

### 2.5 Step 4 — Daily habit
- **Illustration needed?** **Yes.** This is the commitment step. We want a small, warm "you can do this" beat.
- **Scene:** Kō sits beside a **small calendar grid (5×2 mini month view)** with a single day cell filled in amber `#F4A261` — a single mark, not a full streak, because the user hasn't started yet. Above the calendar, a tiny **clock** reads 7:00 (we use 7時 — N5 vocabulary, exists in packs). The visual promise is: "one small block today."
- **Props:** calendar, clock, amber cell. Background: solid `#E0F2FE`.
- **Why this scene:** calendar + 7:00 is a low-friction, non-judgmental "small daily habit" cue. We do not show a streak because the user has not earned one.

### 2.6 Onboarding illustration style rules (binding for Kaisel)
- Flat, no gradients inside shapes (one subtle gradient is allowed in the **background only**).
- Stroke width 0 (we are going full-flat, not outline-flat — outline + flat in 4 illustrations tends to drift between scenes).
- Corner radius on cards: 16 px (matches `ds.radius.md` in our design system). This is a brand decision, not an aesthetic one — illustrations should feel like they belong on the same screens as the cards.
- Two-color rule: brand `#2A6F97` for Kō, amber `#F4A261` for the dot and the one accent per scene. BrandSoft `#E0F2FE` for backgrounds. No third hue.
- All kanji/kana: must be sourced from `src/data/vocabulary/n5.json` and `n4.json` (or wherever the packs live — Kaisel will confirm). **No inventing characters.** The four pieces of text we will use are: 日本語, にほんご, しごと, 7時 — all confirmed N5.
- Each illustration fits a 320 × 280 px artboard, exported as SVG (preferred for crispness) and as 3× PNG (960 × 840) fallback for Android pre-O. We use SVG everywhere possible.

### 2.7 Onboarding summary table

| Step | Illustration? | Mascot scene | Anchor text | Props |
|------|---------------|--------------|-------------|-------|
| 1 Welcome | Yes | Kō on speech bubble | 日本語, あいう | 1 large + 3 small bubbles |
| 2 Language pick | No (inline badge) | Kō head 24 px corner | — | — |
| 3 Workplace goal | Yes | Kō on 3 stacked cards | しごと | 3 cards, 1 hard-hat glyph |
| 4 Daily habit | Yes | Kō beside calendar | 7時 | 1 calendar, 1 clock |

## 3. Empty-State Illustration Plan

The app has 6 candidate screens. We pick **3** to illustrate. The other 3 get a calm typographic empty state with a small inline mascot badge (same as onboarding step 2). The rule: illustrate only when the empty state is the user's first encounter with that screen, and the empty state would otherwise feel like an error.

### 3.1 Home — **Yes, illustrate**
- **Scene:** Kō sits on a large empty card-shaped area, looking up at a small **rising sun** in the corner. One sentence: "Today's lessons will appear here — come back when you're ready." The sun is a single amber circle, simple as a button.
- **Why:** Home is the launchpad. A blank home screen feels broken. One illustration fixes that.

### 3.2 Lessons — **Yes, illustrate**
- **Scene:** Kō is half-hidden behind an open **notebook/book** with hiragana **あ** on the visible page. One sentence: "Pick a unit to start — あ is waiting."
- **Why:** Lessons is where the user does the work. A first-empty state here is high-stakes. The book +  あ tells them exactly what's coming.

### 3.3 Flashcards — **No, typography only**
- **Why:** Flashcards is a feature the user opens deliberately after choosing a deck. By the time they reach it, the deck exists. Empty flashcard state means "no deck selected" — that's a UI problem, not an emotional moment. Use text + inline mascot badge.

### 3.4 Progress — **Yes, illustrate**
- **Scene:** Kō stands next to an **empty bar chart** with three bars of different heights (but all at zero). One amber dot sits at the base of the middle bar. One sentence: "Your progress starts with your first lesson."
- **Why:** Empty progress is psychologically the worst empty state in any learning app. A small visual that says "I will grow into this" matters.

### 3.5 Quiz — **No, typography only**
- **Why:** Quiz is launched from a unit. If empty, the user is in the wrong place. Text instruction is sufficient.

### 3.6 Survival — **No, typography only**
- **Why:** Survival is a phrasebook — by design it has content from the start. Empty state is only "filter returned nothing," which is a search result, not a moment. Text only.

### 3.7 Empty-state summary
- **3 illustrations:** Home, Lessons, Progress.
- **3 typography-only:** Flashcards, Quiz, Survival.
- All three illustrations reuse the Kō silhouette. Total new art effort: 3 SVGs.
- Each illustration must also work in dark-mode-equivalent (we are not in dark mode yet, but we plan for it: the SVG background should be a CSS variable or a token that we can override; the mascot itself stays the same color, the background flips from `#E0F2FE` to `#0F172A` and the surface color goes dark).

## 4. Achievement Badge Plan

The app already has (or will have) eight achievement moments. Each gets a **single monochrome glyph** rendered inside a circular brand-tinted badge (no per-badge color explosion — every badge uses `#2A6F97` background with `#F4A261` accent OR `#F4A261` background with `#FFFFFF` glyph, alternating to give variety without palette drift). The badge frame is the same circle; only the glyph changes. This is the budget-conscious way to do a badge system.

### 4.1 The eight achievements

1. **First lesson completed** — a single **checkmark** inside a circle. Universal "you did it" symbol, the simplest possible first badge.
2. **7-day streak** — a **flame** glyph (one continuous curved shape). Streaks = fire is universal. Amber background, white flame.
3. **30-day streak** — a **calendar with a star** on one cell. The star is amber, the calendar is white-on-brand. The 30-day is a "longer" version of the 7-day, so it should feel like a sibling, not a totally new shape.
4. **First kanji learned** — a single **kanji radical** rendered in white-on-brand. We pick the radical for "person" (亻), which is in N5 (人). Reinforces the lesson.
5. **First 100 words** — a **stack of three small cards** with a tiny "100" hint on the top one. Visual nod to flashcard mechanics.
6. **Level up (N5→N4 or N4→N3)** — an **upward arrow inside a chevron**, three strokes, suggesting progress. Amber background.
7. **Survival section completed** — a **shield with a checkmark** inside. Workplace safety metaphor; signals "you're equipped."
8. **Perfect quiz (10/10)** — **ten small dots arranged in a 2×5 grid** with one amber dot at the end. Abstract, "perfect score" without saying the number.

### 4.2 Badge style rules
- 64 × 64 SVG, viewBox `0 0 64 64`.
- Outer circle: filled brand `#2A6F97` (or warm `#F4A261` on alternating badges for variety), 64 px diameter.
- Glyph: white `#FFFFFF` (or brand-dark `#014F86` on the warm-background badges for contrast).
- Stroke width on glyphs: 4 px equivalent at 64. Thick enough to read at 32 px notification size.
- No text inside badges. The label lives next to the badge in the UI.
- Same circle frame across all eight — variety is only in the inner glyph and the alternating brand/warm background. Two-color rule, applied to the badge system as a whole.

### 4.3 Why 8 and not 12
8 is enough to feel like a system without requiring 12 SVG authoring sessions. The 7-day and 30-day share a family; first kanji and level up share a family (vertical/stack motifs). This visual rhyme cuts perceived complexity in half.

## 5. JLPT Level Badges (N5 vs N4)

**Problem:** N5 and N4 need to be distinguishable at a glance, but feel like one family so the user doesn't feel like they switched apps when leveling up.

**Solution:** Same circular badge frame, same two-color palette, **only the central glyph and the orientation of an internal ribbon change.**

### 5.1 N5 badge
- Circle: brand `#2A6F97` background, white inner border ring.
- Center: a single hiragana character **「あ」** in white, set in a Noto Sans JP weight that matches the design system type scale. A small amber `#F4A261` dot sits at the bottom-right of the circle like a "level pip."
- Visual reading: friendly, first step, "this is where you start."

### 5.2 N4 badge
- Circle: brand `#2A6F97` background (same), white inner border ring (same).
- Center: a single katakana character **「ア」** in white. Same typography, same size, same amber pip.
- **Subtle distinguisher:** the amber pip is moved from bottom-right to **top-right**, and a small **white chevron** is added to the bottom of the badge pointing up (an internal arrow, not a new color). It signals "you went up" without changing the palette.
- Visual reading: same family, slightly elevated.

### 5.3 Why this works
- あ (hiragana) and ア (katakana) are the first characters of each script — instantly readable as "the two writing systems" and as "level 1 / level 2" for any learner.
- The amber pip moving is a one-pixel decision that is the entire differentiator at a glance. It costs nothing.
- The white chevron on N4 is the only structural addition. It is a hierarchy cue, not a new visual language.
- Both badges reuse the badge frame from §4. The whole system (achievements + JLPT levels) is **one circle, two colors, one accent dot, sometimes one chevron.** That is the whole visual system.

### 5.4 Future levels (N3, N2, N1) — out of scope for v1 but documented
If we ship N3 next, the same circle with kanji 一 (one), 二 (two), 三 (three) as the central glyphs, with the chevron+pip position rotating. We do not design them now — the spec is "same family, escalate glyph complexity, move the pip." Kaisel should not pre-author N3+.

## 6. Constraints — Enforcement Checklist

For Kaisel during generation, every asset must pass these:

- [ ] **Mascot is two colors max.** Kō = `#2A6F97` body + `#F4A261` dot. Background = `#E0F2FE`. Nothing else. If an asset wants a third color, we cut a prop.
- [ ] **No invented kanji/kana.** Allowed text across all 25 assets: 日本語, にほんご, あ, い, う, しごと, 7時, ア, 人, 一, 100. Each must be cross-checked against `src/data/vocabulary/n5.json` and `n4.json` before generation. Kaisel does not generate Japanese characters; Kaisel uses these exact strings only.
- [ ] **Palette is the design system palette.** No `text`, no `surface`, no greys — those are UI surfaces, not asset colors. Asset colors: `#2A6F97`, `#014F86`, `#E0F2FE`, `#F4A261`, `#FFF4E6`, `#FFFFFF`. (The soft tints are for backgrounds, the saturated ones for the mark itself.)
- [ ] **Free-tier APIs only.** Allowed: Google Gemini free tier, Bing Image Creator, Leonardo free tier, Ideogram free tier, or local Stable Diffusion if available. **Disallowed:** Midjourney, DALL-E paid, anything > $0/month. Free tier has caveats: ratelimits and watermarks. Kaisel must run all generations twice (a "comp pass" and a "final pass") and pick the cleanest result; watermarks disqualify otherwise.
- [ ] **No brand infringement.** No real product logos in the icon, no public-figure faces, no copyrighted mascots. The asset concept seed mentions "bridge / speech bubble / safety helmet" — those are motifs, not literal trademarks.
- [ ] **Safe zone respected.** Master icon: 824 × 824 inner circle. Android adaptive foreground: 720 × 720 inner safe zone. Splash icon centered with 120 px margin from edges.
- [ ] **Accessibility.** All assets must work in grayscale. The two-color Kō must remain recognizable when the amber dot is removed (silhouette test). If the dot is the only differentiator between Kō and a generic blob, we have failed.
- [ ] **Format consistency.** Icons = PNG (3× scale). Illustrations + badges = SVG with PNG fallback at 3×. Splash = PNG only (Expo doesn't render SVG splash reliably on all Android OEM launchers).

## 8. Recommended Generation Order (for Kaisel)

1. **Kō mascot first.** Author once, reuse everywhere. Get the silhouette right; the rest cascades.
2. **Master icon (1024²).** This is the single highest-leverage asset. App Store judges by it.
3. **Android adaptive parts (fg, bg, mono).** Derived from master, but the monochrome must be re-traced, not just desaturated (desaturated brand blue reads as muddy, not monochrome).
4. **Splash (background + icon).** Splash icon is just Kō centered; background is one gradient.
5. **4 onboarding illustrations (3 with Kō, step 2 is typography only).** Keep Kō's pose and proportions identical across all three; change only props.
6. **3 empty-state illustrations.** Same Kō, simpler scenes.
7. **8 achievement badges.** Quick to author once the badge frame is locked.
8. **2 JLPT badges.** Trivial once the badge frame exists.

This order is dependency-driven: nothing later requires anything earlier to be redone. If we run out of API credits, we still have the highest-priority assets (1–4) done.
